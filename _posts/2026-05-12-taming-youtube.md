YouTube is one of the only social products I still find genuinely useful. It's where I learn how to fix things around the house, where my kids watch a marine biologist explain how octopuses see color through their skin, where I go down rabbit holes on guitar technique or game design. The library is incredible.

But out of the box, YouTube is also a slot machine. Shorts, autoplay, a homepage tuned to whatever is most likely to keep you scrolling, thumbnails engineered in Photoshop to bait the click. The product that *could* be a library is shipped to you as a casino, and that's the version most kids meet first.

A few friends with kids have asked me what I do to make YouTube tolerable in our house. The short version: I treat YouTube the way I'd treat a TV with a hundred channels and a bored producer. I turn off the parts that are designed to hijack attention, and I keep the parts that let me (or my kid) go looking for something specific. Here's the setup.

## Use YouTube on desktop only

This is the first and most underrated rule. I don't have the YouTube app on my phone. If I want to watch something, I have to go to a computer and open a browser. That tiny bit of friction changes everything — YouTube stops being something you reach for in line at the grocery store and becomes something you sit down to use, like a book or a TV.

It also makes every other tip in this post possible. The extensions and ad blockers below are desktop-only. Mobile YouTube is a walled-off experience that Google controls end to end, and they're not going to let you remove Shorts or strip the clickbait thumbnails. If you want YouTube to feel like a tool instead of a feed, the browser on a real computer is where that's actually achievable.

For kids, the same logic doubles. A TV or a family computer is a shared, visible space. A phone is a private one. The setting itself shapes the behavior.

## Turn off the parts designed to be addictive

Most of YouTube's addictive surface is opt-out, not opt-in. Walk through the settings once and a lot of the noise goes away.

- **Turn off autoplay.** There's a toggle right on the player. One video at a time, then a deliberate choice about what's next. This is the single highest-leverage change you can make.
- **Pause watch history.** Settings → Your data in YouTube → YouTube Watch History → Pause. The homepage and recommendations get dramatically quieter, because the algorithm has less to work with. You can still subscribe to channels and watch them directly.
- **Hide Shorts.** Shorts are TikTok grafted onto YouTube and they're the most addictive surface on the site. On the web you can collapse the Shorts shelf; on mobile you can hide it from the home feed in settings. Better, use an extension (below) to remove it entirely.
- **Use Restricted Mode and a Supervised Account for kids.** Not perfect, but it filters a meaningful amount of junk and gives you a content rating to work from.

## Install an ad blocker

This is non-negotiable for me. YouTube ads have gotten longer, more frequent, and more aggressive, and a meaningful number of them are scams, AI slop, or content I don't want my kid to see between a video about volcanoes and a video about sharks.

[uBlock Origin](https://ublockorigin.com/) is free, open source, and the gold standard. Install it in Chrome or Firefox and most of the problem disappears. (On iOS, where extensions are more limited, a content blocker like AdGuard or a network-level blocker like NextDNS is the equivalent move.)

## Use a couple of Chrome extensions to remove the rest

Even with the settings above, YouTube keeps trying to claw your attention back. Two extensions do most of the heavy lifting:

- **[Unhook](https://unhook.app/)** is the one I recommend first. It lets you hide the homepage feed, sidebar recommendations, trending, Shorts, comments, end-screen suggestions — pretty much any element of the YouTube UI you don't want. My setup hides the homepage entirely and the sidebar recommendations. When I open YouTube, I see my subscriptions and nothing else. If I want a specific video, I search for it. If I don't, I close the tab.
- **[DeArrow](https://dearrow.ajay.app/)** (the successor to "Clickbait Remover for YouTube") replaces those engineered thumbnails with the first frame of the actual video, and replaces the YELLING, ALL-CAPS, ARROW-AND-RED-CIRCLE titles with crowdsourced neutral ones. Genuinely transformative — YouTube starts to look like a library catalog instead of a tabloid rack.

Together, those two extensions plus an ad blocker make YouTube feel like a different product. It's calm. It's searchable. It doesn't try to drag you somewhere you didn't ask to go.

## Make subscriptions the front door

The default YouTube homepage is "what the algorithm thinks you'll click." With the settings and extensions above, the front door becomes "channels I deliberately chose to follow." That's a much healthier default — for adults and especially for kids.

Subscribing is the modern equivalent of choosing what magazines to keep on the coffee table. We talk about subscriptions in our house the same way: which creators are worth inviting in regularly, and which ones are fine to watch occasionally but not subscribe to. It turns out an 8-year-old can have a real conversation about this if you frame it right. "Do you want this person in your house every day?" is a surprisingly useful question.

## Why bother

I think a lot about the difference between *pull* media and *push* media. Pull is when you go looking for something — a book, a search, a specific channel. Push is when something is shoved at you — a feed, an autoplay, a notification. Pull media tends to be enriching. Push media tends to be exhausting. YouTube contains both, and the defaults are pushed all the way toward push.

The goal of all of this isn't to make YouTube less fun. It's the opposite. When the autoplay stops and the homepage goes quiet and the thumbnails stop screaming, what's left is the thing that was always good about YouTube: a deep, weird, generous library of people teaching you stuff. That's the version I want my kids to grow up with.

It takes about twenty minutes to set up. It's worth it.
